Fashion has shifted dramatically over the past few decades. From the bold synthetics of the 1980s, to the logo driven 1990s, to the fast fashion boom of the 2000s, each era pushed trends to move faster and clothes to become more disposable. By the 2010s, influencer culture made constant outfit changes normal, accelerating overconsumption. But beginning around 2018, the pattern changes. Thrifting reappears, resale platforms grow, and by 2025, Gen Z has pushed sustainable, secondhand fashion into the mainstream. Why
Sustainable

Gen Z has played a major role in making sustainable fashion more visible. They use social media platforms to share thrift finds, promote secondhand shopping, and celebrate restyled outfits. Online communities on TikTok, Instagram, and Depop make it easy to discover trends, buy and sell items, and participate in fashion culture without relying on fast fashion. This digital influence encourages people to reuse clothing and explore creative ways to express themselves. The rise of secondhand shopping and online resale reflects how Gen Z is redefining modern fashion through sustainability and self expression.

How Did Media Coverage on Sustainable Fashion Change to make it more dominant?

From Newspapers to TikTok: How Media Put Sustainable Fashion in the Spotlight

This timeline shows how media attention around sustainable fashion has grown over time. Early coverage focused on simple reuse like flour-bag clothing, then shifted to environmental awareness in the 1970s, labor ethics scandals in the 1990s, and fast-fashion criticism in the 2000s. By the 2010s and 2020s, YouTube, Instagram, and TikTok made upcycling, thrifting, and circular fashion mainstream. Overall, the media becomes increasingly focused on sustainability leading to the strong interest we see today.
